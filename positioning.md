# Positioning Statement

**Status: 🟡 In progress — first draft (written from belief)**

## What this step is

The positioning statement is the main deliverable of the Discovery stage. It's one tight
paragraph that forces you to answer seven things clearly: *who* the product is for, *what
need* it meets, *what it's called*, *what kind of thing* it is, its *key benefit*, the
*main alternative* people use today, and *how it's different*.

You draft it early from your own belief, then revise it over several passes as research
and interviews come in. The rule: by the time you leave Discovery, every clause has to be
defensible — not just a hunch.

## The results so far

Three of the seven clauses have a first belief written down. The other four are still
blank. Here's the current draft:

> For **5th-grade students learning about water pollution in science class on a school
> Chromebook** (reached through their science teacher, who assigns it),
> who **need to understand and care about water pollution, but find reading-heavy,
> textbook-style materials boring and easy to tune out, so the message doesn't stick**,
> the **Poop Patrol** is a *(product category — TBD)*
> that *(key benefit — TBD)*.
> Unlike *(the main alternative — TBD)*,
> our product *(what makes it different — TBD)*.

### The thinking behind the drafted clauses

- **Target customer.** 5th graders learning about pollution in science class on a school
  Chromebook. Kids this age don't find and adopt educational sites on their own — a
  teacher puts it in front of them — so the teacher is the buyer and the student is the
  user. *(Broader "kids" was rejected as too vague; reaching kids directly at home was
  rejected as unrealistic.)*
- **Need / opportunity.** Plenty of pollution information already exists; the real problem
  is that dense, textbook-style material doesn't hold a 10-year-old's attention, so it
  doesn't stick. The opportunity is engagement and retention, not more facts.
- **Product name.** "Poop Patrol" was chosen to be funny and repeatable, leaning into the
  Tijuana River sewage story. *(Alternatives like "The Yuck River" are on standby in case
  a teacher or school filter finds the name too crude.)*

## What comes next

These beliefs need to be tested — that's the job of the
**[Research]({{ '/research/' | relative_url }})** and
**[Interviews]({{ '/interviews/' | relative_url }})** steps. The biggest open questions:
is water pollution actually in the 5th-grade curriculum, would teachers assign an outside
site, and is engagement really the bottleneck?
