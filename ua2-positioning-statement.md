## Evidence

One subsection per clause. Updated continuously across all five passes.

### Target customer

- **Current belief:** 5th-grade students (~10–11 years old) learning about water pollution during science class, using a school-issued Chromebook. They are the people we want to *reach*. Their science teacher is the gatekeeper who chooses the site and assigns it — the teacher is the buyer/decider, the student is the end user.
- **Basis for the belief:** Student's own intuition about where water-pollution awareness "belongs" — 5th-grade science class. No conversations with teachers or students yet.
- **Evidence found:**
  - (none yet — drafted from belief)
- **Alternatives considered:** "Kids" generally (too broad — a 6-year-old and a 17-year-old need totally different things); reaching kids directly at home (rejected — 5th graders don't find and adopt educational sites on their own; a teacher puts it in front of them).
- **What would change my mind:** If teachers say water pollution isn't part of the 5th-grade curriculum, or that they'd never assign an outside website; or if the natural age for this content turns out to be older/younger.

### Need or opportunity

- **Current belief:** 5th graders need to understand and care about water pollution, but reading-heavy, textbook-style materials don't hold a 10-year-old's attention, so the message doesn't stick. The gap is engagement/retention, not a lack of information existing somewhere.
- **Basis for the belief:** Student's intuition that kids today default to screens over books and tune out dense reading. No teacher or student conversations yet.
- **Evidence found:**
  - (none yet — drafted from belief)
- **Alternatives considered:** Framing the need as "there isn't enough information about water pollution" (rejected — plenty exists; the problem is that it doesn't land with kids). Framing it as "kids don't read books" (that's the mechanism/differentiation, not the underlying need).
- **What would change my mind:** If teachers say their existing materials already engage kids well, or that the real bottleneck is class time / curriculum standards rather than engagement; or if kids actually do absorb the textbook version fine.

### Product name

- **Current belief:** Poop Patrol
- **Basis for the belief:** Chosen to be funny and memorable for 10-year-olds — a name a kid will repeat. Leans into the sewage angle of the Tijuana River story, which is the site's hook.
- **Evidence found:**
  - (none yet — internal choice; thin evidence acceptable for this clause)
- **Alternatives considered:** "Gross Out: The Water Edition," "Holy Crap! A River Mystery," "Sewer Surfers," "Stinky Waters," "The Yuck River" — all in the same funny/gross register; Poop Patrol was the most repeatable.
- **What would change my mind:** If a teacher says the name is too crude to assign in class or wouldn't get past a school filter — then we'd shift to a tamer funny name (e.g., "The Yuck River").

> Mostly internal; thin evidence here is acceptable.
